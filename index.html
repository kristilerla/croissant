A Brief History of Croissants

Origins and Early History

The croissant, a staple of French pastry, has a fascinating history that spans multiple cultures and centuries. Its origins can be traced back to the 17th century, but not in Franceâ€”rather, in Austria.
Austrian Roots (17th Century) Viennese Kipferl: The croissant's precursor is the "kipferl," a crescent-shaped bread or pastry that was popular in Austria. The kipferl dates back to at least the 13th century and was often made with a variety of ingredients, including nuts and sugar. Siege of Vienna (1683): A popular legend attributes the creation of the kipferl to the bakers of Vienna who, while working late at night, heard the Turks tunneling underneath the city walls during the Siege of Vienna. They alerted the city's defenders, and after the siege was lifted, the bakers were honored. They supposedly created the kipferl in the shape of a crescent, mimicking the symbol on the Ottoman flag, as a commemoration of the victory. Introduction to France (18th Century) Marie Antoinette: One theory suggests that Marie Antoinette, originally from Austria, introduced the kipferl to the French court when she married Louis XVI. She is said to have requested the pastries from her homeland, leading to their popularity in France. Early French Variations: French bakers began experimenting with the kipferl, eventually transforming it into the more delicate, flaky version known today.

Evolution into the Modern Chroissant

19th Century Development Viennoiserie: The term "viennoiserie" refers to baked goods made in the style of Vienna, including croissants. In the 19th century, a bakery in Paris called Boulangerie Viennoise, opened by Austrian baker August Zang, played a crucial role in popularizing these pastries in France. Laminate Dough: The key innovation that differentiated the croissant from the kipferl was the use of laminated dough, which involves folding butter into the dough multiple times to create thin layers. This technique results in the croissant's signature flakiness and buttery taste. 20th Century and Global Popularity Mass Production: In the 20th century, advances in baking technology allowed for the mass production of croissants, making them more accessible to a wider audience. Cultural Icon: By the mid-20th century, the croissant had become a cultural icon of French cuisine, symbolizing sophistication and culinary excellence. Its popularity spread globally, and it became a common breakfast item in many countries.
